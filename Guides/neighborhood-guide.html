Claremont Neighborhoods

Mapping Your Vibe

Claremont isn't just one "City of Trees" — it's a collection of distinct neighborhoods that cater to very different versions of the California dream. Whether you're chasing the intellectual hum of a college town or the quiet of a mountain trailhead, where you land in this city dictates your daily rhythm.

The Village & Surrounds

The historic Village and its surrounding blocks are the heartbeat of the city — walkable, lively, and close to everything.

Claremont Village

Claremont's walkable downtown hub. A mix of bungalows and historic homes surround the main streets, where you'll find the best concentration of restaurants, coffee shops, and independent boutiques in the area. High walkability, constant energy, and the closest thing Claremont has to urban living.

Old Claremont

The historic residential core surrounding the Village and Memorial Park. Diverse architectural styles — Craftsman bungalows, Spanish Revival, mid-century — on tree-lined streets. Centrally located with expansive green spaces, sports fields, and a neighborhood feel that draws long-term residents who want quiet streets close to everything.

College Park

Directly adjacent to the Claremont Colleges, this neighborhood is popular with faculty, staff, and families who want the campus atmosphere without living on it. Tree-lined streets, historical charm, and an intellectual energy that feels baked into the sidewalks.

Arbol Verde

One of Claremont's oldest neighborhoods, listed on the National Register of Historic Places. Dating to the 1910s, it features charming early-20th-century homes with a deep sense of history. A quiet, walkable pocket near the Village with real heritage.

North Claremont: The Foothills

Cross north of Foothill Boulevard and the energy shifts. Wide, quiet streets, mature tree canopies, and the San Gabriel Mountains filling the horizon.

Claremont Hills / Padua Hills

The luxury tier. Custom homes with expansive mountain views, quiet surroundings, and a sense of seclusion that feels miles away from the Village below. Claremont Hills Wilderness Park is essentially your backyard. Padua Hills is home to the historic Padua Hills Theatre, a Claremont landmark since 1927.

Claraboya

A premier foothill neighborhood with custom-built estates and stunning mountain views. Homes here range from $1.5M to $2.5M+, with large lots and a secluded, resort-like feel. One of the most sought-after addresses in Claremont.

Blaisdell Ranch

An upscale neighborhood in the northeast corner bordering the Wilderness Park. Spacious properties — typically 3,800 to 5,900 sq ft — on wide lots with mature landscaping and a deep sense of privacy. The Blaisdell Ranch Preserve adds to the natural setting.

Piedmont Mesa

In north-west Claremont, Piedmont Mesa features spacious ranch-style homes on generous, tree-filled lots. A well-established family neighborhood with a relaxed, suburban feel and easy access to both the foothills and the Village.

South Claremont

South of the Village, the pace slows down. More affordable, family-friendly, and full of hidden character.

Russian Village District

One of Claremont's most historically significant neighborhoods, located along South Mills Avenue. Listed on the National Register of Historic Places since 1978, these 15 folk-architecture cottages were built during the Great Depression by Konstanty "Steve" Stys, a Polish immigrant, using salvaged materials. Despite the name, the builder was Polish — the "Russian" name stuck from the era. A true hidden gem with storybook charm.

South Claremont

A quiet suburban area offering a mix of mid-century and contemporary homes near city parks and everyday amenities. More affordable than the north side, with easy freeway access and a neighborhood feel that's family-friendly and low-key. Less foot traffic, more front-yard space.

Insider Tips

The altitude shift: North Claremont sits at a higher elevation. It often feels 3-5 degrees cooler in summer and offers clearer views of the winter snowcaps on Mount Baldy.

Transit strategy: Living near the Village gives you walkable access to Metrolink — you're at LA Union Station in under an hour, no freeway required.

School zones: Your neighborhood determines your elementary school. Research Claremont Unified School District boundaries before you sign a lease or make an offer.

The price gradient: Generally, the further north and higher in elevation you go, the higher the home prices. South Claremont and areas near the 10 freeway tend to be the most budget-friendly entry points into the city.
