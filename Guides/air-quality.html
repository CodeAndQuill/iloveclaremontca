Claremont Air Quality & Fire Watch

Real-time AQI for 91711, Red Flag Warnings, and the local playbook for smoke days — because inland foothill air behaves nothing like coastal LA.

Claremont sits in a very particular slice of the South Coast Air Basin: the inland smog belt, tucked right up against the Angeles National Forest. That geography drives two air-quality stories our coastal neighbors never see. Summer ozone regularly climbs into "Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups" territory in July, August, and September as pollution drifts inland from LA and cooks in the afternoon sun. And when the Angeles NF burns — Bobcat 2020, Ranch2 2020, and the smaller Line Fire scares since — smoke reaches us hours before it reaches Long Beach. This page is the local reference: a live AQI reading for 91711, the practical playbook for bad-air days, and links to the tools worth trusting.

Why Claremont's air is different

The same trees that make our streets beautiful also trap the pollution that arrives on the sea breeze. Understanding the local pattern is half the battle.

The inland smog belt

Pollution generated across the LA Basin gets pushed east by the ocean breeze every afternoon. It piles up against the San Gabriel foothills — and Claremont, Upland, and Rancho Cucamonga sit right where it collects. That's why our summertime ozone regularly runs higher than Pasadena, higher than downtown LA, and dramatically higher than the coast.

Summer ozone (July – September)

Ozone peaks between roughly 3–7 PM, after a full day of sunlight has cooked the incoming pollution. This is the wrong window for heavy outdoor exercise at Thompson Creek Trail or the Wilderness Park loops. Mornings and late evenings are typically fine even on bad-ozone days.

Wildfire smoke and PM2.5

When the Angeles NF or a foothill fire ignites, fine-particle smoke can hit Claremont within an hour or two. PM2.5 is the dominant risk here — the microscopic particles that lodge in your lungs. During active fire events, watch the AQI hourly, not daily.

Santa Ana winds

Dry offshore winds from the desert flip our normal pattern. On Santa Ana days the smog blows out to sea — visibility is spectacular — but fire danger spikes dramatically. Almost every catastrophic Southern California fire has happened during a Santa Ana event.

Fire season for Claremont

Claremont's proximity to the Angeles National Forest means a Red Flag Warning is worth paying attention to. Here's what the terminology actually means, and the fires that reset the neighborhood's memory.

What is a Red Flag Warning?

The National Weather Service issues Red Flag Warnings when a combination of low humidity, dry fuels, and strong winds means any spark can become a rapidly-spreading fire. It's not a prediction that a fire will happen — it's a warning that if one starts, conditions favor it running fast. During a warning: avoid outdoor burning, don't park hot cars on dry grass, and know your evacuation route.

Fire Weather Watch

The precursor to a Red Flag Warning — conditions are expected to develop within 12 to 72 hours. Treat it as a two-day heads-up to review your family's go-bag, check that important documents are gathered, and top off gas tanks.

Recent local memory

The Bobcat Fire (September 2020) burned 116,000 acres in the Angeles NF and blanketed the whole San Gabriel Valley in smoke for weeks — Claremont AQI stayed in the "Unhealthy" and "Very Unhealthy" range for the better part of a month. The Ranch2 Fire (August 2020) hit Azusa Canyon just west of us. Every summer since has had at least one smoke-drift day worth staying inside for.

What to do when the air is bad

A practical, do-this-first playbook for smoke days and high-ozone afternoons.

  1. Move exercise indoors or to before 10 AM. Ozone doesn't peak until afternoon; morning walks and runs are usually fine even when the day's forecast is grim. During active wildfire smoke, skip outdoor exercise entirely regardless of hour.
  2. Close windows, run the AC on recirculate. Your central AC system uses a filter — upgrading to a MERV 13 or higher during fire season pulls PM2.5 out of your air. Regular MERV 8 filters barely help with smoke.
  3. Run a portable air purifier in the bedroom. Look for HEPA units with a CADR (Clean Air Delivery Rate) matched to your room size. A $150 unit in a bedroom is more impactful than upgrading the whole-house filter alone.
  4. N95 or KN95 if you must go outside. Cloth and surgical masks don't filter PM2.5. Real N95s from the pandemic drawer still work if the elastic hasn't perished.
  5. Watch kids and older adults closely. The very young and very old show air-quality symptoms first — coughing, headache, tight chest. Trust those signals; the AQI number is a proxy, not a diagnosis.

Where to check yourself

The tools that actually work for our part of the map — bookmarked in the order you'll use them.

AirNow.gov — Claremont

The EPA's official AQI reading, pulled from regulatory-grade monitors in the San Gabriel Valley (Glendora, Pomona, Azusa). Updates hourly. This is the number to trust when the neighborhood argues about it.

Watch Duty

The gold-standard wildfire tracker for California foothills. Run by a nonprofit staffed by fire reporters and radio scanners — you get near-real-time incident maps, evacuation orders, and confirmed sightings before mainstream news. Free iOS and Android app. If you keep only one fire-safety tool on your phone, make it this one.

NWS Los Angeles/Oxnard Forecast

Federal weather forecast pinned to Claremont's coordinates — the source of Red Flag Warnings, Fire Weather Watches, and wind advisories. If a warning is active for our county, it lights up at the top of the page.

South Coast AQMD

Our regional air quality regulator. Sign up for their alert system to get email or text notifications when air quality advisories are issued for the San Gabriel Valley. Their smoke-advisory alerts arrive faster than the national feeds.

City of Claremont Emergency Preparedness

The city's official emergency-alerts page — sign up for local mass-notification alerts for evacuation orders, shelter openings, and closures.

Sensitive populations in Claremont

A quick mental map of who feels the air first, and where they are in town.

5C students new to Southern California

Every fall the 5Cs bring in students from cleaner climates — the Pacific Northwest, New England, international. Their lungs aren't calibrated to our summer ozone or fire smoke. A moderate AQI day feels worse to a first-year from Portland than to a lifelong resident. If you know an incoming freshman, share the AirNow app.

Pilgrim Place & Mt. San Antonio Gardens

Two of Claremont's older-adult communities. Both maintain their own indoor air handling, but on very bad smoke days it's worth a phone check to a neighbor to make sure they're staying inside and comfortable.

CUSD schools

Claremont Unified follows the state's outdoor-activity-restriction guidelines based on AQI. During Red Flag Warnings or heavy smoke, expect recess and PE to move indoors. Check the district's parent portal for the day's status.

The quick-check cheat sheet

Right now: AirNow — Claremont for the AQI number. Watch Duty for active fires.

Red Flag Warning active? Skip outdoor burning, know your evacuation route, keep your phone charged.

Smoke rolling in? Close windows, AC on recirculate, kids and elders inside, purifier on in the bedroom.

Summer afternoon ozone spike? Move the trail run to sunrise, not sunset.
