Jacaranda Season in Claremont

When They Bloom & Where to See Them

For a few weeks every spring, Claremont trades its green canopy for electric violet. The sidewalks turn purple, the streets look like movie sets, and everyone suddenly becomes a photographer. Here's your guide to the bloom — when to go, where to look, and what the locals know.

A Tree from the Amazon

The Jacaranda (Jacaranda mimosifolia) feels like it belongs in Southern California, but it actually comes from the mountain valleys of Argentina and Bolivia. Horticulturalist Kate Sessions — known as the "Mother of Balboa Park" — first brought them to San Diego in the late 1890s. By the 1930s they had spread to Los Angeles, and urban planners adopted them for the 1950s "Los Angeles Beautiful" campaign, using that high-voltage purple to break up the uniformity of the suburbs. Claremont's jacarandas are a living piece of that mid-century vision.

When to Catch the Peak

The window: Late April through early June.

The sweet spot: Mid-May is usually the pinnacle — the canopy is at its thickest, just before the flowers start dropping and carpeting the streets in purple.

The trigger: Jacarandas wait for a consistent stretch of heat before blooming. A cool spring pushes the peak later.

Best Streets for Photos

Yale Avenue

Start at the Claremont Depot and walk north along Yale. The purple blooms against the 1920s Village architecture is the classic shot.

Harvard Avenue

One block east of Yale, Harvard runs parallel with its own canopy of jacarandas. Great for a quieter stroll with fewer crowds.

8th Street (Between Yale & Harvard)

The street is narrow enough that branches meet in the middle, creating a natural violet tunnel. Perfect for portraits.

The Packing House

The industrial-chic background against soft purple blossoms makes for a sharp, modern contrast.

Scripps College Campus

The gardens and Mediterranean-style buildings framed by jacarandas might be the most photogenic spot in all of Claremont. Open to visitors.

Why So Many Jacarandas?

They're not an accident — jacarandas are a deliberate part of Claremont's urban forest. They thrive here because they're remarkably drought-tolerant once established, and they shed their leaves right before blooming, so nothing competes with the color.

After the Walk

Since the best streets are all in the Village core, your photo walk doubles as a 3–4 mile loop if you start at Memorial Park and wind through to the Packing House. Cool down afterward at OONA Boba House — signature milk tea, a window seat, and time to scroll through your photos.

Insider Tips

Don't park under one. The flowers attract aphids that leave a sticky residue on your car's paint. Use the parking structures instead.

Shoot at golden hour. 30 minutes before sunset, the low light makes the purple pop against white bungalows and Spanish tile roofs.

Bring allergy meds. The pollen is real. If you're sensitive, mornings before the wind picks up are your best bet.
